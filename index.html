Urgency

discription

This pattern creates a sense of urgency or pressure to make the user act quickly, without giving them enough time to think or compare options. It often uses countdown timers, limited-time offers, or phrases like “hurry up” or “don’t miss out”.

example

A travel website shows a message saying “Only 2 rooms left at this price” and a timer counting down from 10 minutes1

Scarcity

discription

This pattern creates a sense of scarcity or exclusivity to make the user feel that they are missing out on a valuable opportunity if they don’t act fast. It often uses indicators of low stock, high demand, or limited availability.

example

An online store shows a message saying “Only 3 items left in stock” and a badge saying “Popular”3

Misdirection

discription

This pattern uses visual or verbal cues to direct the user’s attention away from something important or to something they may not want. It often uses colors, sizes, positions, or wording to make the desired option more prominent or the undesired option less noticeable.

example

A software installer shows a button saying “I accept these terms” for an unrelated program, while the option to skip installing it is much smaller and greyed out

Social Proof

discription

This pattern uses social influence or peer pressure to make the user conform to the behavior or opinion of others. It often uses testimonials, ratings, reviews, endorsements, or statistics to persuade the user.

example

A product page shows a message saying “4.5 out of 5 stars from 1,234 reviews” and a banner saying “As seen on TV”

Obtruction

discription

This pattern makes it difficult or inconvenient for the user to perform a certain task, such as cancelling a subscription, deleting an account, or opting out of a service. It often uses multiple steps, hidden options, confusing language, or delays to discourage the user.

example

A website shows a message saying “If you would like to cancel your membership, please call … or contact us via email at …”, instead of providing a cancel button.

Sneaking

discription

This pattern tries to hide, disguise, or delay the disclosure of information or actions that the user may not agree with or notice. It often uses pre-selected checkboxes, fine print, or additional costs or items to trick the user.

example

A shopping website adds an extra item or a warranty to the user’s cart without their consent or knowledge.

Force Action

discription

This pattern forces the user to perform an action or provide information that is not required or relevant for their current task. It often uses pop-ups, interstitials, or mandatory fields to interrupt or block the user.

example

A website asks the user to sign up for a newsletter or create an account before they can access the content or service they want.
